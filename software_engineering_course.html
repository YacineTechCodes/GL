01 · Core Concepts

What is Software Engineering?

Before defining Software Engineering (GL), let's understand its two components.

🖥️ What is Software?

  • Programs (executable code)
  • Data (inputs, databases)
  • Documents (design, architecture, test reports, user manuals, installation guides…)

⚗️ What is Engineering?

  • Developing products using well-defined scientific principles
  • Satisfying constraints of cost, quality, security
  • Applied in many sectors: civil, electrical, aeronautics, mechanical…
💡 Core Idea: Apply classical engineering methods and discipline to the field of computer software development.

Four Definitions of Software Engineering

01

A set of methods, techniques and tools dedicated to the design, development and maintenance of software.

02

A discipline of computer science that groups knowledge, processes, and scientific achievements for the design, implementation, verification, and documentation of software with the aim of optimizing its production and quality.

03

Using engineering principles to obtain economical, reliable and efficient software on real machines.

04

The discipline that applies engineering methods to design, develop, maintain and test software that should be reliable, efficient, and tailored to user needs.

Objectives & Challenges

💰
Optimize Cost & Time
Deliver software within the agreed budget and schedule.
👥
Meet Customer Needs
Build what the client actually needs, not just what was initially stated.
📅
Meet Deadlines
Deliver on time through proper planning and tracking.
🏆
High-Quality Software
Ensure performance, reliability, and security throughout the product lifecycle.
🔧
Ease of Maintenance
Build software that is easy to correct, update, and scale over time.
Key Takeaway

Software Engineering applies structured methods, tools, and processes to build software that is reliable, efficient, on-budget, and meets real user needs — not just code, but programs + data + documentation.

Qualities of Software

Good software isn't just about working code. It must meet a broad set of quality criteria — and these criteria sometimes conflict with each other.

Validity

The software meets user needs and performs its intended functions.

Reliability

Results are accurate and consistent — output is as expected.

Robustness

Operates correctly even under abnormal conditions (bad input, failures).

Performance

Fast response times and efficient use of system resources.

Maintainability

Easy to correct, modify, or update when requirements change.

Extensibility

New features can be added without redesigning the entire system.

Reusability

Components can be reused, in whole or in part, in other applications.

Efficiency

Reasonable consumption of memory, CPU, and other resources.

Portability

Can run on different hardware or software environments with minimal effort.

Integrity

Protects itself and its data against unauthorized access or modification.

User-Friendliness

Easy to learn and use: clear UI, shortcuts, helpful error messages.

⚠️ Trade-offs exist!

These factors are sometimes contradictory. For example, high performance might require sacrificing portability. The right compromise depends on the application context — a video game prioritizes performance; a banking app prioritizes security and reliability.

03 · Context

Environment & Actors

Software doesn't exist in isolation — it interacts with its environment and involves multiple human actors.

📌 Environment = All entities that may interact with the software without being part of its internal code.
👤
Users
General public (text editor), doctors (diagnostic systems), developers (IDE)…
💾
Other Software
Booking platforms, DBMS, operating systems, APIs and external services.
🖥️
Hardware
Sensors, drones, screens, autonomous cars, robots, RAM, CPU…

Who is Involved?

🏦

Customer (Owner)

The person or organization that orders and finances the software. They define the requirements.

🙋

User

The person who actually uses the software. May be different from the customer (e.g., employees of a client company).

👨‍💻

Developer

The person or team responsible for designing and programming the software.

🏢

Subcontractor

An external company or person who performs part of the work under contract from the main developer.

04 · History
4 min read

The Software Crisis

Around 1970, researchers observed a troubling pattern: software projects were consistently failing. This led to the birth of Software Engineering as a formal discipline.

⚠️ Symptoms (1970s)

  • Delivery deadlines not met
  • Budgets consistently exceeded
  • Failure to meet user/client needs
  • Difficult to use, maintain, and upgrade

🔍 Root Causes

  • Software size and complexity
  • Large, poorly coordinated teams
  • Lack of design/verification methods
  • Neglect of validation phases
  • Poor understanding of client needs
  • No client involvement during development
🔑 Key Principle: A failure is always caused by a fault (in the specification, code, or communications). However, a fault doesn't always cause a visible failure.

Famous Incidents

1962 · Space

Mariner Probe Lost

NASA's Mariner probe to Venus was lost in space due to a single bug in a FORTRAN guidance program — a missing hyphen in the code.

1981 · NASA

Space Shuttle Columbia Delayed

The first launch was delayed by two days due to a synchronization bug between 5 computer systems, written in the HAL/S language designed by NASA.

1996 · Europe

Ariane 5 Explosion — $500 Million

The Ariane 5 rocket exploded 36 seconds after takeoff, costing $500 million. Cause: an integer overflow — speed values exceeded the 16-bit data type used in the navigation software.

2000 · Worldwide

Y2K Bug — $600 Billion

The Year 2000 bug caused a global panic. Systems stored years as 2 digits; "00" could be interpreted as 1900. Correction costs in the USA alone reached ~$600 billion (Gartner Group).

Project Success Rates (Standish Group, 2013)

✅
Successful
Completed on time, within budget, with all requested features. ~39% of small projects
⚠️
Mixed
Completed but delivered late, over budget, or missing features. ~52% of large projects
❌
Failed
Abandoned before completion or delivered but never used. ~38% of large projects fail
Key Takeaway

Software failures are caused by faults in requirements, design, or code — not just bad luck. Getting the requirements right (only 6% of effort, but 56% of errors!) is the single most impactful thing a team can do.

05 · SDLC
5 min read

Software Development Process

A structured process schedules a set of activities to produce software systematically.

Core Activities

1

Analysis & Specification

Capture requirements and clearly describe what the software must do. Produces the Cahier de charges (SRS — Software Requirements Specification).

2

Design

Elaborate a concrete solution: system architecture, modules, data structures, algorithms. Blueprint before building.

3

Implementation

Program the designed solution in a chosen programming language. Translates design into executable code.

4

Verification & Validation

Verification (internal): Does the software meet its specification? Validation (external): Are the customers satisfied?

5

Delivery

Installation, deployment, and configuration of the software in the target environment.

6

Maintenance

Correction of bugs and evolution of features. Studies show maintenance consumes ~67% of total development effort!

📊 Key Statistic (Zeltovitz & De Marco): Although requirements definition takes only 6% of development effort, it is the source of 56% of all errors and 82% of maintenance costs. Getting the requirements right is critical.

Development Models

🌊 Cascade (Waterfall)

  • Each phase must be fully completed before the next begins
  • Each step produces a document for the next
  • Errors often detected late (during testing or delivery)
  • Correction may require re-checking all phases

✔️ V-Model

  • Each development phase has a corresponding testing phase
  • Enables early error detection
  • Suitable for medium-complexity projects requiring reliability
  • Tests are planned in parallel with development phases

🌀 Spiral

  • Risk-driven iterative approach
  • 4 phases per cycle: plan → identify risks → develop → review
  • Produces prototypes progressively
  • Good for large, complex, high-risk projects

⚡ Agile (e.g., Scrum)

  • Short iterative development cycles (sprints)
  • Continuous client involvement and feedback
  • Flexible response to changing requirements
  • Working software delivered frequently
Key Takeaway

No single model is perfect. Waterfall is rigid, V-Model adds testing, Spiral manages risk, and Agile embraces change. Choose based on project size, risk level, and how well you understand requirements upfront.

06 · Requirements

Functional vs Non-Functional Needs

Requirements in an SRS (Software Requirements Specification) are classified into two categories.

⚙️ Functional Needs (WHAT)

  • What the system must do
  • Actions, behaviors, and features
  • If missing, the user cannot complete a task
  • Example: "A patient can book an appointment online"

📊 Non-Functional Needs (HOW)

  • How the system should behave
  • Performance, security, availability, constraints
  • Quality attributes and operational constraints
  • Example: "Medical data must be encrypted"
